---
title: Sometimes the Harshest Things People Say are the Nicest
desc: An essay on why you should listen.
---

In February of 2021, I was ready to take the AMC 10A. I had been
mocking high scores of 120+ the few months before, so I was rather
confident I would do well.

I entered the test, got through the first 5-10 problems or so fairly
well. Then, problems 12-16 pretty much killed me (or, more like
two problems in that range). One was some combinatorics problem
disguised as algebra, another was some problem involving cones
that I somehow got horribly confused on. I started frantically
jumping back and forth, then tried to move on, but I saw
"Problem 19" followed by a series problem and quickly got too
scared and went back to those two problems.

Needless to say, I definitely did not get 120+ on that test.
However, I knew I was definitely borderline on qualifying.
It was pretty much 50-50, as I forgot if I had sillied 
one of the problems or not.

Turns out I sillied the combo problem, and I also
ended up sillying P9 because I accidentally forgot
to carry a 1 in one of my equations. It would have been
fine if I made AIME, but the cutoff was 103.5, and I 
scored a 97.5; just one question away, but now that
meant I had to wait another year, all because of one silly
mistake... (though now I know that's not true,
and sillies can be avoided and my training was horrible).

I don't want to talk about the 10B. I simply
treated it like a "second chance" as if I was already
at an AIME qualifier level, barely studying at all;
I managed to silly an impress 4 problems out of 16 attempted
(or something like that), scoring an 84.

Afterwards, I just completely lost it. I cried about
how all I wanted was to make AIME, how one thing,
so unfair, caused me to lose it all, wait another
year, as if my precious time had been wasted...

It wasn't even like I didn't like math, I wasn't
just doing it for my parents, I wasn't forcing myself
to do it, so why did this have to happen to me?

My dad shouted at me, but I wasn't even doing it for him!
I didn't feel like he needed to care, but nevertheless
he spoke how he felt. Telling me to man up, be the 
teenager I was, and stop crying. He told me
how I never really studied, how I wasted most
of my time and only did one practice test a week,
how I spent most of my time watching YouTube,
and I couldn't help but think, "all of this is true";
I knew there was no point to argue.

All of it was true though; I wasn't just passively
agreeing because I was emotionally broken. I
accepted everything he told me, all the emotion
he used that day, because it only showed that
*he cared*. I cared to listen.

Another anecdote: My mom's college professor
could not help her on one of the topics that she
needed to research on. Luckily, at a conference she
went to, she came in contact with an expert in the field
she was researching, a professor from New Zealand who
was willing to take her in. He easily lost his temper,
often said things in the most direct ways,
and calling his students stupid was common. Needless
to say, my mom was one of his only students! (though
of course, she only could meet with him online,
so it probably wasn't as bad). However, my mom
trusted him and knew he was just doing a favor for her;
the paper he helped her write became one of her best.

So, the next time someone insults me, I know
to understand and to listen: "maybe they
are saying something that's true and that I need to change".
Doing so, I always try not to make the same mistake twice.
