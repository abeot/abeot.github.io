---
title: Regrets
desc: Having to choose between different things
---

I started thinking about colleges over quarantine after taking
some courses offered by Princeton, when I start dreaming about
how to get employed begin a software engineer and how getting
into a good college could help in that matter.

To me, the most important part of getting into a good college
were the awards and extracurriculars, so I tried to identify
my interests at the time:

- Math (including the AMCs, although I wasn't that good I enjoyed it)
- Coding (encompassing competitive programming and also programming projects)
- Fencing (my athletic aspect)
- Cello (my music)

I thought this would work out; after all, MIT has four spots.
However, this changed pretty fast.

## Dropping Cello

This really hurt for me, because music has always been one of my 
passions. I've always had perfect pitch and also sing a lot
(I'm not good at it and it's pretty embarassing, but it's a way
of expression!) Cello really meant something to me, because
that's where I made some of my closest friends; Tyler and Johan
were my buddies, and us three even played a trio last year!
My private teacher was such a cool and nice guy (shoutout to
Dr. Kozlov)! I attribute a lot of my success to him, because
he helped me appreciate the love for the instrument and almost
made it feel like I was walking in his footsteps. Cello being
part of an orchestra was just another way to meet other people,
and over the course of a few years I made my fair share of friends
and quite a few people knew me as well. (I also want to comment
that I did have just *one* relationship with someone I met at
All State, and we're still friends).

It's also just the fact that music is something that cannot be
expressed in any other way. It tells a story of itself and expresses
the inexpressible; there's just so much creativity, and so 
much human feeling happening, and I loved the cello so much 
because of how I felt playing it; almost transcended. I
didn't care how I looked when I played it, and all the body 
language, vibrato, and movement happening was just so natural.

The reason I had to drop it was because of a conflict. I 
made our county's High School GT Orchestra as a freshman,
which is really, really impressive; if I continued, I'm quite
confident I could have made senior All State. However,
it conflicted with fencing practice. So basically, I had to 
choose between cello and fencing, and I chose fencing.

Although I really do enjoy both very much, I think I made
the final choice because of what it looked like on paper.
People probably couldn't tell I genuinely loved it,
and employers and AOs probably didn't care; for the latter,
they probably even saw it as just me being another sweaty asian.
Just because I don't want to lose my love of music, I'm thinking
of just picking up the guitar and trying to play a few songs
(and maybe even make embarassing videos, lol), but I'm not sure
if I have the time right now. On the other hand, fencing was
much more interesting; it's such a unique sport and I was
nationally ranked pretty high, so it looks so much better.

I feel like a bigger factor in why I ultimately chose cello
is because of my parents. I know they haven't really cared about
my cello, and don't really mind my achievements; rather, they
focus a lot of time and money on my fencing, and I really don't
want that to go to waste. I feel like it's really ironic
how that seems to be the only extracurricular they care about,
and I pretty much have to do everything else by myself (and
am decently good for return-of-investment), but I don't
want to disappoint them. I like things more when I'm more
in control of my growth, and maybe that's why I've thought
of fencing more as a routine then a hobby.

However, fencing is just about as useless as cello in life.
Sure, maybe the AOs are impressed, but it's the same thing
with employers. It's a good way to get fit, but rather than spending
10 hours a week, I can spend an hour a day to just work out
(to be far a good part of that 10 hours does come from driving).

Another thing about fencing is that how I got into it was
pretty ironic; I had played soccer, swimming, tennis, and 
probably a lot more sports before, and each one of them
I didn't make the travel team, the select team, or whatever.
When my mom decided to choose fencing since she heard it from a 
friend, we did fairly well in our first competition, and so I
guess she thought it was a talent she needed to take advantage of.
Hence the large amount of time and money my mom and my dad have 
spent on it, along with a fair bit of struggles when I haven't done well.

So, what happens to that last spot on my extracurricular list?
Turns out, my mom wants me to consider competitive programming
and programming as two different things. Honestly, I'm fine with
that, and after doing a bit of real-world programming last summer
(data analysis in Julia), I'm pretty hyped for another similar project.

Still, I'm just sad that I had to drop cello this year; my orchestra
teacher even told me he would put me in the highest orchestra if I
signed up, but my goal is to be a computer science major, so I
won't be able to go to Kentucky with the rest of my friends.

And a fair bit of warning: don't drop things because
of how other people think of them, try to make the assessment
yourself.
